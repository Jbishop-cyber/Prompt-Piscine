# Objective

## Strengthen weak prompts using patterns.

### Step 1: A vague prompt

```
Prompt:

Write abour inflation
```
```
Output:

Inflation is essentially the economy's way of telling you that your money doesn't go as far as it used to. It’s the steady increase in the prices of goods and services across the board. When inflation hits, each dollar (or euro, or yen) you hold buys a smaller percentage of a product than it did yesterday. Think of it less as prices going up and more as the purchasing power of your currency going down.
Why Does It Happen?

Economists generally point to three main culprits behind rising prices:

    Demand-Pull Inflation: This is the too much money chasing too few goods scenario. When consumer demand outpaces what companies can produce, prices get bid up.

    Cost-Push Inflation: This happens when the cost of production rises. If the price of oil or raw materials spikes, companies pass those costs onto you to maintain their profit margins.

    Built-In Inflation: Also known as a wage-price spiral. As living costs rise, workers demand higher wages. To pay those wages, businesses raise prices, creating a loop.
```

### Step 2: Instructional or Comparative prompts

- Instructional prompt

```
Prompt:

Write a 200-word explanation of inflation for high school learners.
```
```
Output:

Think of inflation as a slow-motion change in the "size" of your money. It isn’t just about the price of a single video game going up; it’s a broad increase in prices across the entire economy. When inflation occurs, your purchasing power;the amount of stuff one dollar can buy;shrinks.

The Two Main Drivers:

    Demand-Pull: This happens when everyone suddenly has extra cash and wants to buy the same popular sneakers. If demand for those sneakers is higher than the store's supply, the store raises the price. It’s too much money chasing too few goods.

    Cost-Push: This occurs when it becomes more expensive for companies to make products. If the price of electricity or gasoline spikes, a bakery has to charge more for bread just to cover their bills.

Why it Matters:

Economists generally aim for a Goldilocks's rate of about 2%. This is enough to encourage people to spend money now rather than hoarding it, but not so high that it makes basic needs unaffordable. If inflation gets too high, the government’s Central Bank will usually raise interest rates to cool things down, making it more expensive to borrow money for cars or college.
```

- Comparative Prompt

```
Prompt:

Compare the effects of inflation in urban and rural areas in 5 bullet points
```
```
Output:

While inflation hits everyone's wallet, the flavor of the struggle changes depending on your zip code. Here is how the effects differ between urban and rural settings:

    Transportation Costs: In rural areas, residents often face a higher inflation tax on travel. Because public transit is rare and distances to jobs or groceries are long, rising gas prices hit rural households much harder than urban dwellers who might have access to subways, buses, or walkable neighborhoods.

    Housing vs. Land Values: Urban areas usually see sharper spikes in rent, making city living quickly unaffordable during inflationary periods. Conversely, in rural areas, inflation can lead to rising land and commodity values, which may benefit property owners and farmers even as their operating costs (like fertilizer) go up.

    Access to Goods and Competition: Urban consumers benefit from high retail density. If one grocery store raises prices too high, there are five others nearby. In rural areas, there may only be one "general store," meaning residents have no choice but to pay whatever the local price hike dictates.

    Wage Growth Disparities: Historically, wage growth;which helps offset inflation;tends to happen faster in urban centers with diverse job markets. Rural economies, often tied to specific industries like agriculture or manufacturing, may see wages stagnate while the national cost of living continues to climb.

    Utility and Infrastructure Expenses: Urban utilities are often more regulated or shared across a dense population. In rural areas, the cost of heating (often relying on propane or heating oil) and maintaining long-range infrastructure can see more volatile price swings than the standardized electric grids found in cities.
```

### Step 3: Document the improvement in clarity and usefulness of the outputs.

- In Clarity:

The vague prompts were weak and produced broad responses, while the instructional prompt clearly defined the audience, the number of words and focus, which made the output easier to understand, but the comparative prompt place the focus into categories, thereby allowing the reader to quickly understand the differences between both.

- In Usefulness:

The vague prompt's output is less practical for teaching, especially high schoolers, but the instructional prompt produce an output that is suitable for teaching, since it's targeted at high schoolers, while the comparative prompt provided information that can be used directly for analysis, presentation and decision-making.
