# Objective

## Practice creative writing through prompts

### Step 1: Choose a theme
```
Tourism
```

### Step 2: Write a simple generative prompt

```
Prompt:

Write a short story about tourism in under 300 words.
```
```
Output:

The guidebook promised "undiscovered serenity," but as Elena stepped off the ferry, she found a neon-lit landscape of selfie sticks and overpriced gelato. For three days, she followed the blue dots on her digital map, ticking off cathedrals and plazas like items on a grocery list. She saw the world through a five-inch screen, capturing "perfect" moments she didn't actually feel. On the fourth morning, her phone died.
Panic flared, then faded into a strange, quiet curiosity. Without the algorithm’s guidance, she wandered away from the main thoroughfare. She turned down an alley draped in laundry that smelled of lavender and woodsmoke. At the end sat a tiny bakery with no sign and only three chairs.
An old man, his hands dusted with flour, handed her a warm pastry without a word. Elena sat by the window, watching a stray cat chase shadows across the cobblestones. There were no plaques to read, no hashtags to append, and no crowds to elbow.
She realized then that tourism was about the destination, but travel was about the detour. She didn't take a photo of the golden crust or the way the light hit the ancient stone. Instead, she just ate, tasting the rosemary and the quiet, finally arriving at the place she had traveled three thousand miles to find.
```

### Step 3: Test with at least two different themes

- Input 1

Choose a theme
```
Love
```

```
Prompt:

Write a short story about love in under 300 words
```
```
Output:

Arthur found the note tucked inside a library book he hadn't opened in forty years. “Meet me at the clock tower if you decide to stay,” it read, in Clara’s looping, frantic script.
He remembered that day vividly—the rain slicking the pavement, his suitcase heavy with the weight of a predictable future elsewhere. He had waited at the station, not the tower, convinced that love was a grand gesture he wasn't brave enough to make. He had spent decades wondering if she had shown up.
Now, his fingers traced the yellowed paper. Arthur grabbed his coat and walked toward the center of town. The clock tower was a relic now, surrounded by glass offices and coffee shops, but it still chimed with a heavy, rhythmic heart. He sat on a bench, feeling foolish. He was an old man chasing a ghost.
"You're late," a voice rasped.
Arthur looked up. She was wrapped in a thick wool scarf, her hair a silver halo. She held a book of her own, the spine cracked and worn.
"I came every year on this date," Clara said, sitting beside him. "Just for an hour. I figured eventually, you’d run out of excuses to be anywhere else."
He didn't apologize. There were no words for forty years of silence. Instead, he simply reached out and took her hand. Her skin felt like parchment, fragile and familiar. In the quiet space between them, the "grand gesture" finally arrived;not as a beginning, but as a long-awaited return.
```

- Input 2

Choose a theme
```
Death
```

```
Prompt:

Write a short story about death in under 300 words
```
```
Output:

The Old Man didn’t knock; he simply stepped through the kitchen wall as Silas was boiling water for tea. He wasn’t a skeleton in a hood, but a soft-spoken fellow in a tweed jacket, smelling faintly of rain and old libraries.
"Is it time?" Silas asked, his voice steadier than he expected.
"It is," the Old Man replied, pulling a silver pocket watch from his vest. "But the water is nearly bubbling. It would be a shame to waste it."
They sat together at the small wooden table. Silas poured two mugs of chamomile. For a long time, there was only the sound of sipping and the rhythmic ticking of the wall clock. Silas thought he would feel terror, but instead, he felt a strange, sweeping lightness—the way a student feels when the final bell of the year rings.
"Does it hurt?" Silas whispered.
"Only the leaving," the Old Man said kindly. "The arriving is like laying down a heavy pack after a very long walk."
Silas looked around his kitchen—the chipped mugs, the sunlight hitting the flour dust, the life he had built. He realized he wasn't afraid of what was coming; he was just saying goodbye to what had been.
When the mugs were empty, the Old Man stood and held out a hand. Silas took it. His grip was warm and firm.
As they stepped through the wall together, Silas didn't look back. He was too busy noticing that the hallway was suddenly filled with the scent of spring, and for the first time in years, his knees didn't ache.
```

### Step 4: Comparing the outputs

Each prompt generated different outputs depending on the theme chosen. For instance, when the theme was about tourism, the story focused more on discovery and a quiet reflection on the emotional meaning of traveling; for the love theme the story became more emotional, focusing on reunion and human connection, while the theme about death gave a more calm and figurative tone, by representing death as a peaceful transition rather than something to be afraid of. This just shows that generative prompts can actually influence the writing style, emotions and can even change the direction of the narrative.
